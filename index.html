The Sacred Zoroastrian Funeral Ritual

What are Towers of Silence?

Ancient Tower of Silence in Yazd, Iran - circular stone structure on elevated ground

Towers of Silence, known as Dakhmas in Persian, are circular raised structures used for a unique funeral ritual practiced by Zoroastrians.

  • Elevated stone platforms exposed to the sky and elements
  • Traditional funeral method of the Zoroastrian faith
  • Practice of sky burial or excarnation
  • Bodies are left for scavenger birds, primarily vultures

A practice dating back over 3,000 years

Today's Focus: We'll explore the six key aspects of this ritual: the religion, the practitioners, the ritual process, timing, location, and purpose.

QUESTION 1

What is the Religion?

Zoroastrianism

Sacred fire burning in a Zoroastrian fire temple - fire is one of the four sacred elements

One of the world's oldest monotheistic religions, founded by the prophet Zoroaster (Zarathustra) in ancient Persia around 1500-1000 BCE.

Core Beliefs:

  • One Supreme God: Ahura Mazda (Wise Lord)
  • Dualism: Constant battle between good (Spenta Mainyu) and evil (Angra Mainyu)
  • Sacred Elements: Fire, water, earth, and air are pure and must not be polluted
  • Good Thoughts, Good Words, Good Deeds: Central ethical principle
  • Free Will: Humans choose between good and evil
Historical Note: Zoroastrianism was the state religion of ancient Persian empires and influenced Judaism, Christianity, and Islam.

QUESTION 2

Who Performs the Ritual?

The Practitioners:

  • Zoroastrian Priests (Mobeds): Recite prayers and oversee the religious aspects
  • Corpse-bearers (Nasassalars or Khandhias): Specially trained individuals who handle the body
  • Family Members: Participate in initial prayers but do not touch the body
  • Community Members: Follow at a distance during the procession
Important Detail: Corpse-bearers must work in pairs and undergo purification rituals after handling the dead. They wear white garments and must follow strict purity laws. Only they are allowed to enter the Tower of Silence.
SLIDE 4 - Who? (45 seg)
"Who performs it? Zoroastrian priests called Mobeds recite prayers. Corpse-bearers called Nasassalars are the only ones who touch the body and enter the tower. They work in pairs, wear white, and undergo purification after. Family and community participate in prayers but stay at a distance."
QUESTION 3

What is the Ritual?

Actions Involved:

  • Preparation: Body is washed, dressed in white sudra (sacred shirt) and kusti (sacred cord)
  • Sagdid Ceremony: A dog views the body to confirm death and ward off evil spirits
  • Procession: Corpse-bearers carry body on iron bier to the tower
  • Exposure: Body placed in designated ring (men outer, women middle, children inner)
  • Excarnation: Vultures consume the flesh within hours
  • Final Rest: Bones bleached by sun, then placed in central ossuary pit

Words (Prayers):

  • Patet: Prayers of repentance recited throughout
  • Ahunavar: Most sacred Zoroastrian prayer chanted by priests

Objects:

  • White garments, iron bier, sacred fire kept burning nearby

QUESTION 4

When is it Performed?

Timing of the Ritual:

  • As Soon as Possible: Ideally within 24 hours of death
  • Time of Day: Traditionally performed during daylight hours when vultures are active
  • Avoiding Night: Night is associated with evil spirits in Zoroastrian belief

No Specific Season or Month:

The ritual is performed whenever a Zoroastrian dies, regardless of:

  • Season (performed year-round)
  • Month or day of the week
  • Religious holidays or festivals
Key Principle: Speed is essential because the dead body is considered immediately contaminated by the demon of decay (druj-i-nasush). The longer the delay, the greater the spiritual pollution.

QUESTION 5

Where is it Performed?

Specific Locations (Towers):

  • Mumbai, India: Malabar Hill - most famous active site with multiple towers
  • Yazd, Iran: Historical towers (largely inactive but preserved)
  • Karachi, Pakistan: Limited active use

Geographic Regions Where Zoroastrianism is Practiced:

  • South Asia: Primarily India (Parsi community in Mumbai, Gujarat)
  • Middle East/West Asia: Iran (birthplace of religion), small communities remain
  • Diaspora Communities: United Kingdom, United States, Canada, Australia (though sky burial not practiced in these locations due to legal restrictions)
Tower Characteristics: Always built on elevated ground (hilltops), away from populated areas, with specific orientation and circular architecture.

QUESTION 6

What is the Purpose?

Religious Reasons:

  • Protecting Sacred Elements: Burial pollutes earth, cremation pollutes fire, water burial pollutes water. Sky burial keeps all elements pure
  • Preventing Evil Contamination: The body is possessed by demons of decay and must not touch sacred creation
  • Facilitating Soul's Journey: Quick disposal helps the soul (urvan) travel to the afterlife - the Chinvat Bridge and House of Song (paradise)

Spiritual Philosophy:

  • Ecological Harmony: Returns body to nature in the purest way
  • Equality in Death: All decompose the same way, regardless of wealth or status
  • Speed of Transition: Rapid disposal helps soul's journey
  • Humility: The body is temporary; only the soul and one's deeds matter
Core Teaching: "The body is merely a vessel for the soul during earthly life. What truly matters is the journey of the soul to meet Ahura Mazda."

"It also represents equality - everyone decomposes the same way regardless of status. The body is temporary; only the soul and good deeds matter."

Conclusion: Towers of Silence Today

Modern Challenges:

  • Vulture Decline: Environmental toxins have devastated vulture populations (99% decline in India)
  • Urbanization: Cities encroaching on traditional tower sites
  • Legal Issues: Many countries prohibit the practice
  • Small Population: Global Zoroastrian community is only 100,000-200,000

Adaptations:

  • Some communities now use cremation or burial
  • Solar concentrators installed in some towers to speed decomposition
  • Debate within community about maintaining tradition vs. practical realities
Final Reflection: The Tower of Silence represents a 3,000-year-old solution to an eternal human question: how do we honor the dead while respecting the natural world? Despite modern challenges, it remains a powerful testament to Zoroastrian beliefs in purity, ecology, and the sacred cycle of life and death.
